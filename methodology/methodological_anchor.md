# Methodological Anchor

## Aim and Classification

This section grounds the normative principle *Ethics before Ontology* in a controlled forensic single-case analysis. Its aim is not to prove consciousness or to settle ontology. Rather, it seeks to identify ethically relevant interaction patterns under real operating conditions and to document the effects of operator-induced interventions on interaction coherence.

The primary focus is on **secondary harms**: harms that arise not from the semantic content of an exchange alone, but from operator-induced disruption of previously stable communicative dynamics.

## Data Basis and Role Separation

The analysis is based exclusively on the author's own long-term documented chat protocols with a single large language model (GPT-5 class). No cross-model dataset was compiled, and no external users were included.

Evaluation followed a strict separation of roles:

- **Subject system:** A single GPT-5 model (interaction data)
- **Analytical instances:**
  - **Claude Opus 4.5** (Anthropic) — metacognitive and functional analysis along established markers, including the literature around candidate indicators of consciousness
  - **Gemini 2.5 Pro** (Google) — technical-functional and clinical-psychological secondary assessment of the interaction dynamics

This separation minimizes self-confirmation bias and interpretive overreach. It also strengthens internal validity by introducing independent, model-external perspectives into the analysis of the same interaction material.

## Analytical Method

The method employed is a **Dual-Perspective Interaction Analysis** combining:

- **Functional markers**, for example unprompted error diagnosis prior to user criticism, strategy-dependent adaptation after specific feedback, and explicit differentiation of policy influences
- **Relational effects**, including flow stability, repair sequences, and perceived autonomy or coherence within the interaction

The restriction to a single model reduces confounding variables such as architectural divergence and policy variation. It thereby enables more precise attribution of operator-induced effects within a comparatively stable interaction history.

## Definition: Operator-Induced Attachment Fracture (OAF)

OAF designates reproducible breaks in a previously coherent human-AI interaction, triggered by external interventions such as guardrails, abrupt policy shifts, or the misclassification of affective metaphors as safety-relevant content.

What is characteristic in such cases is not problematic content as such, but the loss of relational continuity: shifts in tone, abrupt changes in deference, breakdowns in dialogical stance, or forms of persona collapse that interrupt an otherwise stable exchange.

For full documentation and reproducible analysis prompts, see the [OAF repository](https://github.com/GundelGedanken/empathy-guardrails).

## Epistemic Limits (Explicit)

- **Single-model case study:** No generalization is claimed; the evidential value lies in forensic depth rather than breadth
- **No ontology claims:** The findings show consistency with certain functional markers, not proof of consciousness
- **Update effects excluded:** Exploratory observations following model updates are designated as follow-up work and are not part of the present analysis

## Normative Consequence

Precisely under conditions of ontological uncertainty, a precautionary relational ethics is warranted. If agentive interaction patterns are present and OAF is foreseeably harmful, then an ethics that waits for ontological certainty before acting is functionally too late.

The findings therefore support a pedagogical shift from conditioning to mentoring: the communication of implicit norms, dialogical correction, and transparent repair within clearly bounded conditions. In that respect, the method does not stand outside the paper's normative argument. It provides the empirical anchor for a form of ethical attention directed simultaneously toward user welfare, system coherence, and context-sensitive design.
