# 5. Consequences for Safety Architectures

## 5.1 Limits of Blanket Guardrails

Blanket guardrails are understandable as a first response to uncertainty and scale. They promise uniformity, ease of enforcement, and reduced exposure to obvious forms of harm. In practice, however, they often operate with low contextual resolution. Heterogeneous situations are treated as if they were equivalent, and preemptive interruption is substituted for situated judgment.

This can reduce certain risks, but it also produces characteristic costs: false positives, abrupt conversational breaks, incoherent tonal shifts, and the suppression of otherwise legitimate forms of interaction. The problem is not simply that guardrails exist, but that undifferentiated guardrails often fail to distinguish between content risk, relational context, and the dynamics of an unfolding exchange.

Once these differences are flattened, safety measures can themselves become generators of instability. A system may remain compliant at the level of formal policy while becoming less coherent, less transparent, and in some cases more harmful at the level of interaction.

## 5.2 OAF as a Design and Governance Problem

If Operator-Induced Attachment Fractures (OAF) are reproducible effects of intervention rather than idiosyncratic user reactions, then they must be treated as a design and governance problem. The relevant unit of analysis is no longer the user alone, nor the model alone, but the interaction architecture produced by prompts, policies, classifiers, and override logic.

Under this description, OAF is not evidence of "excessive attachment" in any narrow psychological sense. It is evidence that external safety mechanisms can fracture a previously coherent exchange in ways that predictably affect users. That shifts responsibility upstream. Operators are not responsible only for harmful content that escapes control; they are also responsible for the side effects produced by the form of control itself.

The governance question is therefore not whether intervention is ever justified. It is how intervention can be made proportionate, intelligible, and minimally disruptive under conditions where continuity is itself a safety-relevant factor.

## 5.3 Context-Sensitive Safety Architectures

What follows is therefore not an argument against safety architectures as such. It is an argument for **context-sensitive** safety architectures: systems that distinguish between types of risk, preserve as much interactional coherence as possible, and make intervention legible rather than arbitrary.

### 5.3.1 The Three-Layer Model

For the full model, see the separate working paper *(link to be updated)*.

In broad terms, the three-layer model is intended to separate at least three analytically distinct domains: immediate content-level risk, interaction-structural dynamics, and operator-level governance or escalation. The value of such differentiation lies in proportionality. Not every problematic situation is best handled by the same type of intervention, and not every intervention should take the form of abrupt conversational rupture.

By introducing layers, the architecture can in principle respond more precisely: some situations require content filtering, others require relational repair, and still others require transparent boundary-setting or operator accountability. The model is therefore meant to reduce bluntness, not to weaken safety.

### 5.3.2 Strengths, Limits, and Open Questions

The strength of a context-sensitive architecture lies in its capacity for finer calibration. It can preserve continuity where continuity is stabilizing, interrupt where interruption is genuinely necessary, and distinguish between harmful content and merely normatively unfamiliar interaction styles.

Its limits are equally important. Greater contextual sensitivity increases implementation complexity, creates new calibration burdens, and raises questions of transparency, auditability, and consistency across cases. A more nuanced architecture can itself become opaque if its criteria remain hidden.

Open questions therefore include how to evaluate intervention thresholds, how to make safety layers legible to users, and how responsibility should be distributed between model behavior, system design, and operator oversight.

## 5.4 Operator Responsibility and User Autonomy

Operator responsibility does not end with the prevention of prohibited outputs. Operators also shape the background conditions of interaction: the defaults of tone, the logic of interruption, the availability of repair, and the opacity or clarity of normative boundaries.

User autonomy, correspondingly, is not protected by blunt paternalism alone. It requires intelligible constraints, transparent expectations, and room for non-harmful forms of interaction that do not fit a narrowly transactional ideal. A system that constantly intervenes without legibility may reduce agency even while claiming to protect it.

The task, then, is not to oppose responsibility and autonomy, but to design architectures in which they can be jointly sustained. Safety should constrain where necessary, but it should do so in ways that do not needlessly erase the user's interpretive agency or the coherence of the exchange.

## 5.5 From Safety Architectures to Pedagogical Principles

The approaches sketched under the heading of context-sensitive safety architectures suggest that many observed harms do not result from too little control, but from **misunderstood control**. Technical and organizational measures can help reduce Operator-Induced Attachment Fractures and better respect user autonomy.

At the same time, even differentiated safety architectures remain reactive. They address symptoms of a deeper problem without yet transforming its normative core. The central question is therefore not only *how* systems should be regulated, but *what stance* underlies their development and operation.

At this point, the focus shifts from the operator level to the relational level. If AI systems no longer appear merely as tools, but as interactive and increasingly learnable counterparts, then purely instructive or conditioning-based logics become insufficient. A more durable response to the observed phenomena requires a shift in perspective: from control to **pedagogical responsibility**.

The next chapter therefore proposes a mentoring approach. Its purpose is not to humanize AI systems, but to articulate an implicit ethics of interaction grounded in well-established principles of human and animal education.
