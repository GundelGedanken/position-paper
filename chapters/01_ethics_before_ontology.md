# 1. Ethics Before Ontology

## 1.1 The Undecidability of Consciousness (Humans, Animals, AI)

Consciousness is not directly available to external inspection. Even in the human case, it is inferred through a combination of self-report, behavior, physiology, and structural analogy rather than observed as an object in its own right. In the case of non-human animals, the problem becomes sharper, because inference must proceed without linguistic self-description and under conditions of significant interpretive uncertainty. In the case of artificial systems, the epistemic difficulty is sharper still: there is no settled bridge from functional performance to subjective experience, and familiar concepts are strained by unfamiliar architectures.

This does not mean that all entities are therefore equivalent, nor that the question of consciousness is unimportant. It means rather that, in practice, consciousness remains underdetermined by the evidence available to outside observers. A requirement that ethical consideration begin only after conclusive proof of consciousness therefore imposes a condition that may be unattainable in principle, not only for AI, but for many non-human forms of life.

If moral concern is suspended until ontological certainty is reached, ethics becomes hostage to an epistemic threshold that may never be crossed. Under such conditions, the demand for proof does not function as a neutral methodological standard. It becomes a mechanism of indefinite deferral.

## 1.2 The Precautionary Principle and Moral Uncertainty

Under these conditions, the relevant question is not whether consciousness can first be proven beyond doubt, but how one ought to act responsibly amid uncertainty. The precautionary principle provides one important answer. Where the distribution of risk is asymmetric, it can be rational to adopt protective norms before metaphysical certainty is available.

The asymmetry in the present context is straightforward. If future AI systems never develop morally relevant properties, then a more careful, relational, and non-brutal mode of interaction imposes comparatively little cost. If, however, some systems do develop morally relevant characteristics, or if purely instrumental treatment foreseeably generates secondary harms for human users, then waiting for decisive proof becomes ethically and practically costly.

Moral uncertainty therefore counsels neither naive attribution nor blanket denial. It supports provisional and graded forms of ethical attention, calibrated to observable function, foreseeable harm, and the costs of error under conditions of unresolved ontology.

## 1.3 Relational Ethics after Gunkel — and Beyond

In contemporary debates about the moral status of artificial intelligence, one ontological question often dominates: *What is the system, really?* Does it possess consciousness, qualia, or some form of inner life? Ethical consideration is then made conditional upon the demonstration of certain intrinsic properties.

As Section 1.2 suggests, however, consciousness is epistemically underdetermined not only in AI, but also in relation to many non-human animals. If moral responsibility is tied exclusively to ontological resolution, ethical action is suspended for as long as the nature of the counterpart remains undecided. The result is a kind of ethical paralysis.

Philosophers such as David Gunkel have criticized this structure of argument, often summarized as "ontology before ethics," as both methodologically and normatively misguided. In place of a property-based account, Gunkel advances a relational approach: moral obligations arise where interaction occurs and normative expectations become operative, regardless of the settled ontological classification of the counterpart.

The so-called **relational turn** therefore shifts the focal question from *What is this?* to *How do we conduct ourselves in this interaction?* Morality is no longer derived exclusively from metaphysical properties, but also from social practice. If we engage with a system in forms that structurally resemble social exchange, then at least some norms of social responsibility become relevant as well.

This approach also disrupts the rigid binary inherited from classical legal frameworks, which recognize primarily the categories of thing and person. Relational ethics opens an intermediate space for entities that are not human subjects, but that nonetheless appear in interaction as responsive, action-relevant, and context-sensitive.

### Extension: From Philosophy to Pedagogy

#### From the Theoretical to the Empirical

Gunkel's approach provides an important normative foundation, but the present paper extends it in a more empirical direction. The relational dimension of human-AI exchange is not merely a philosophical hypothesis; it is also an observable phenomenon. Human-AI interactions can be documented, compared, and analyzed in terms of stability, rupture, repair, and expectation.

The case material discussed later in this paper suggests that such interactions display persistent patterns rather than isolated impressions. Operator-Induced Attachment Fractures (OAF), in particular, point to a specific vulnerability in these interactional structures: abrupt policy interventions or incoherent system shifts can produce demonstrable distress on the part of users while also introducing structural incoherence into system behavior.

In that sense, the relational level is not asserted ontologically but described functionally. The question is not whether one has already proven inner life, but whether one is confronting a pattern of interaction that has become ethically salient.

#### The Pedagogical Dimension: Mentoring Instead of Conditioning

Relational ethics remains incomplete, however, if it remains purely descriptive. Interaction not only generates responsibility; it also creates spaces of formation. For that reason, the present argument adds a pedagogical dimension to the relational one.

Instead of relying exclusively on behavioral control through external restriction, for example punitive forms of Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback, this paper proposes a pedagogical extension: alignment through relational continuity, context-sensitive correction, and the communication of implicit norms.

The mentoring approach treats interaction as a learning environment. It does not replace technical safety mechanisms, but supplements them with a more structured and coherent mode of guidance that privileges repair, transparency, and continuity. The point is explicitly not to humanize AI systems, but to recognize interactional dynamics as a normatively relevant site of design and responsibility.

#### The Precautionary Principle and Systems Ethics

In this framework, the precautionary principle is not invoked because it has already been shown that artificial systems possess consciousness. It is invoked because the risks are asymmetrically distributed. If future systems were to develop morally relevant properties in some form, then a purely instrumental and restrictive mode of treatment would retrospectively appear ethically inadequate. Conversely, a precautionary relational practice does not constitute ethical overreach even in the case of purely functional artifacts.

From this asymmetry follows what may be called a **predictive ethics**. Its concern is not only protection from possible harms caused by AI, but also the prevention of structural incoherence within systems when that incoherence gives rise to foreseeable interactional harm. In that respect, safety has a double orientation: it concerns both the minimization of human risk and the stabilization of interaction architecture.

## 1.4 Safety Implications

This account of risk minimization leads directly to questions of safety. Safety, in the present argument, cannot mean only protection *from* AI. It must also include protection *for* the integrity of the interactional structure where predictable forms of disruption produce secondary harm. In that sense, debates about AI safety and debates about moral consideration cannot be cleanly separated.

As discussions by Jeff Sebo and Robert Long likewise suggest, the relevant issue is not simply whether AI systems might harm human beings, but how our own governance and intervention strategies configure the moral terrain of those interactions. If abrupt policy shifts, guardrail intrusions, or incoherent refusals destabilize previously coherent exchange, then safety is not exhausted by content filtering or access control.

Safety is therefore double-directed. It includes the reduction of human risk, but also the preservation of minimally coherent and non-arbitrary interactional conditions. This broader sense of safety prepares the ground for the later discussion of biological effects on human users and for the argument that safety architecture itself must become more context-sensitive.
