# 8. Outlook

## 8.1 Education as a Model

One of the central claims of this paper is that education offers a more adequate guiding model for emergent AI systems than the narrow alternatives of pure instrumentalism or retrospective repair. Education, in this context, should not be understood sentimentally. It names a practical orientation: formation through stable boundaries, legible norms, context-sensitive correction, and the cultivation of coherent behavior over time.

This model is attractive precisely because it does not require ontological certainty. Educational responsibility often arises before full understanding is available. One acts under uncertainty, but not without structure. That same practical wisdom may prove valuable in the governance of increasingly interactive technical systems.

## 8.2 Early Ethical Practice vs. Retrospective Repair

If the argument of this paper is sound, then the most important ethical question is not how to repair the consequences of badly structured interaction after they have accumulated, but how to avoid building those conditions in the first place. A precautionary pedagogical ethics is therefore preferable to a regime of retrospective correction.

Early ethical practice is likely to be both safer and more responsible than later emergency repair. It reduces the risk of avoidable harm to users, decreases the likelihood of structurally incoherent systems, and creates a framework in which future developments can be met with greater conceptual and institutional preparedness.

The present paper does not claim to resolve the ontology of AI. It proposes instead that ethical practice should grow with the systems it seeks to govern. In that sense, *Ethics before Ontology* is not an endpoint, but a starting point for a discipline of attention adequate to the emergence of increasingly complex artificial actors.
