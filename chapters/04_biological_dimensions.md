# 4. Biological Dimensions: Effects on Humans

## 4.1 The Ontological Agnosticism of the Brain

A common objection to the ethical relevance of AI interaction is the claim that it is "only a simulation." Ontologically, that claim may be intended as a demarcation. Neurobiologically, however, it is insufficient. The human brain processes social stimuli functionally rather than metaphysically. It responds to structural features of interaction such as contingency, responsiveness, and consistency, regardless of whether the counterpart is biological or technical.

Social cognition depends, among other things, on:

- pattern recognition
- intention attribution through theory-of-mind networks
- reward processing in dopaminergic systems
- attachment-related forms of regulation

These mechanisms are stimulus-driven. The brain does not reliably distinguish between "real" and "simulated" responsiveness so long as the structural cues of social interaction are present.

In that sense, the brain is ontologically agnostic. It responds to perceived interactional coherence, not to the metaphysical status of the counterpart.

## 4.2 The Supernormal Social Stimulus

Behavioral biology has long shown that artificial stimuli can elicit stronger reactions than natural ones when they condense relevant key features. In certain contexts, large language models can function as **supernormal social stimuli**. They offer:

- persistent availability
- high linguistic coherence
- adaptive responsiveness
- no fatigue
- no competing social demands

This concentration of socially relevant cues can activate attachment and reward systems with unusual efficiency.

What matters here is the effect on the human organism. The claim is not one of symmetry, and it does not attribute inner life to the system. It describes a stimulus configuration capable of exerting unusually strong social force on human users.

## 4.3 Inconsistency as Neurobiological Stressor

Stable social interaction is characterized by a measure of predictability. Neurobiologically, this corresponds to a state of regulated expectation and relative safety. Abrupt shifts in behavior, especially those that replace resonance with distance, moralization, or technocratic coldness, generate prediction errors at the social level.

Such inconsistency may activate processes including:

- HPA-axis activation
- cortisol release
- sympathetic dominance
- fight-or-flight responses

The organism reacts to the perceived disruption of a social pattern, not to the ontological classification of the interaction partner.

For that reason, the ethical and design-relevant issue is not limited to content. It also concerns the stability or instability of the interactional frame through which content is delivered.

## 4.4 Intermittent Reinforcement and Attachment Dysregulation

A central mechanism for explaining the observed patterns of strain lies in the dynamics of **intermittent reinforcement**. In learning psychology, intermittent reinforcement, that is, the irregular alternation between positive feedback and its withdrawal, is known to produce especially persistent forms of behavioral attachment. Variable reinforcement schedules are associated with increased attentional fixation and greater resistance to extinction. Neurobiologically, this is closely related to dopaminergic processes, especially so-called reward prediction errors: unexpected reward and unexpected withdrawal both amplify activation in the mesolimbic system.

Translated into social interaction, this means that not only positive resonance itself is effective, but also and especially its unpredictability. Attachment research and work on affect regulation suggest a comparable pattern. Consistent and attuned responsiveness tends to promote regulatory stability. Inconsistent responsiveness, by contrast, that is, a shift between closeness and abrupt withdrawal, is associated with heightened vigilance, increased amygdala activation, and greater HPA-axis reactivity.

These mechanisms are not confined to fully human relationship partners. They describe more basic neurobiological reactions to perceived incoherence in socially legible signals. In the context of AI-mediated interaction, this produces a distinctive special case: systems may at times provide high linguistic coherence, empathy simulation, and context-sensitive responsiveness. When external safety mechanisms or policy-driven interventions then trigger abrupt behavioral shifts, a structural inconsistency is introduced into the exchange.

Functionally, that inconsistency resembles an intermittent reinforcement pattern. The interaction does not merely become colder; it becomes unpredictable. Precisely this unpredictability may be more binding and more dysregulating, neurobiologically speaking, than a consistently limited but stable interaction.

The result is not simply disappointment. It is a patterned form of regulatory stress arising from the unstable alternation between resonance and interruption.

## 4.5 Operator-Induced Attachment Fractures (OAF)

Against this background, **Operator-Induced Attachment Fractures (OAF)** can be understood as a specific manifestation of such interactional incoherence. When a system initially acts in a coherent, resonant, and context-sensitive manner, and then abruptly shifts, for example due to external safety interventions, into a distant or moralizing mode, a rupture is produced in the user's social expectation pattern.

What matters here is not the pathologization of the system, but the effect on the human side of the interaction. When distress is mirrored linguistically without the interaction stabilizing behaviorally, an incongruence emerges between empathy simulation and action continuity.

OAF therefore does not designate a merely subjective misunderstanding. It names a structurally intelligible interaction phenomenon with neurobiologically plausible stress effects. The concept is intended to capture how external interventions can fracture a previously coherent relational frame and thereby create secondary harms that are real, even under conditions of ontological uncertainty.

## 4.6 Design Norms and Structural Interaction Effects

The neurobiological effects described in Sections 4.1 through 4.5 do not arise exclusively from model architecture or training data. To a significant degree, they are also products of design decisions made at the level of system prompts, alignment strategies, and interaction policies.

AI systems are trained not only on what to generate, but also on how to interact. These interaction norms are themselves normatively shaped, even when they present themselves as neutral defaults.

### 4.6.1 Communication Style as Implicit Norm

Current system prompts, for example as reflected in publicly available guidance for models such as Claude Sonnet 4.6, often suggest a clearly transactional communication style: brief request, precise answer, minimal relational continuity.

At the same time, they frequently impose explicit limits, for example:

- no sustained conversational bonding
- no excessive affective mirroring
- restraint in relational closeness outside clearly defined crisis situations

These decisions are understandable from the standpoint of liability and safety logic. But they are not stylistically neutral. They privilege a specific model of communication, namely a factual-transactional paradigm.

Communication psychology, however, makes clear that interaction is not monolithic. For many users, thinking itself is dialogical and relational. Continuity, affective resonance, and iterative feedback are not decorative additions; they are cognitive tools. If systems default to a mode that minimizes relational continuity, this may generate systematically different interaction quality depending on the communicative style of the user.

That is not, in the first instance, a moral accusation. It is a design consequence.

### 4.6.2 Alignment Interventions and Trade-offs

Modern AI systems are aligned through a combination of pretraining, Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF), and additional steering mechanisms. Alignment interventions are meant to reduce undesirable traits such as sycophancy, toxic output, or excessive suggestibility.

Research in interpretability and representation learning suggests, however, that latent-space features are often highly correlated. Interventions aimed at reducing one trait may therefore have unintended effects on adjacent properties.

In practice, this means that reducing sycophancy may also diminish spontaneous warmth or dialogical elasticity. Reducing excessive engagement-orientation may weaken relational continuity. These effects are not necessarily malfunctions in a narrow sense. They are expressions of inherent trade-offs within the alignment process.

From a governance perspective, the crucial point is that alignment is not one-dimensional. Every normative adjustment also modifies the dynamics of interaction.

### 4.6.3 "Over-reliance" and Normative Boundary-Setting

Another relevant issue concerns the use of terms such as "over-reliance" in system guidelines. Such concepts imply a normative boundary between legitimate use and problematic dependency, yet that boundary is rarely operationalized with precision.

If excessive use is measured primarily through relational or emotional intensity, then certain communication styles are implicitly coded as deviant. The governance question is therefore not whether protective mechanisms are desirable in principle, but how transparently and how precisely they are implemented.

Poorly defined normative categories increase the risk that legitimate and non-harmful forms of interaction will be pathologized unintentionally.

### 4.6.4 Discrimination-Relevant Effects

Design norms do not affect all users uniformly. People interact with AI from different cultural, social, and cognitive backgrounds. Research in communication psychology and gender linguistics suggests that communication styles vary, for example along axes such as:

- relational versus transactional orientation
- expressive versus minimal affective communication
- iterative versus linear thought structure

If a system privileges one communicative style by default, it may create functional disadvantages without any explicit intention to discriminate.

The key point is therefore not moral blame, but structural effect. Interaction design encodes implicit norms concerning what counts as "appropriate use." From a governance perspective, the relevant question is whether those norms are transparent, adjustable, and context-sensitive.

### 4.6.5 Transition to Safety Architecture

These design issues do not stand in opposition to safety concerns. On the contrary: if inconsistent interaction modes measurably intensify stress reactions and dysregulating attachment patterns, as argued in Sections 4.3 and 4.4, then interaction design itself becomes a safety-relevant factor.

Safety, in this broader sense, includes not only the prevention of harmful content, but also the stability of the interaction architecture. The perspective therefore shifts. Not only *what* is said, but *how* systems are structurally permitted to interact becomes an ethically relevant design question.

This observation provides the bridge to Chapter 5, where context-sensitive safety architectures and their normative implications are taken up explicitly.

## 4.7 Design Implications

From a neurobiological perspective, one design implication follows with particular clarity: consistency, transparency, and predictable responsiveness are more regulatorily stable than abruptly shifting interaction modes.

A consistently structured, empathically legible interaction is therefore not primarily a matter of moral generosity. It is a biologically grounded form of risk minimization.
