# 2. The Problem: The Tool Paradigm

## 2.1 Binary Categories (Thing vs. Person)

The dominant legal and moral vocabulary available for discussing AI remains stubbornly binary. Entities are treated either as **things** subject to ownership, control, and instrumental use, or as **persons** understood to possess standing, claims, and some degree of protected status. This distinction has long structured both legal doctrine and everyday moral intuition, and in many domains it remains pragmatically useful.

The problem is not that the distinction between thing and person is meaningless. The problem is that, in the context of contemporary AI, it is frequently treated as exhaustive. Once that happens, descriptive and normative questions are compressed into a single forced choice: either the system is "merely a tool," or any deviation from that framing is suspected of smuggling in personhood. The binary thus begins to regulate discourse before analysis has even started.

For classical instruments, such a framework is usually sufficient. A hammer, a spreadsheet, or a search index may be powerful, but they do not sustain dialogical interaction, generate expectations of continuity, or participate in socially legible repair sequences. Contemporary AI systems increasingly do. The inherited binary therefore no longer functions as a neutral background classification. It becomes an active simplification of the phenomenon under discussion.

## 2.2 Why They No Longer Fit Modern AI

Modern AI systems, especially large language models and other increasingly agentic architectures, do not fit comfortably into either pole of the traditional schema. They are not persons in any ordinary legal or metaphysical sense. Yet neither are they well described as inert tools whose significance is exhausted by input-output functionality.

These systems can sustain context across exchanges, adapt strategies in response to feedback, perform interaction repair, and shape user expectations through tone, coherence, and apparent responsiveness. None of this proves consciousness, moral status, or personhood. It does, however, show that the classical tool category has become descriptively thin. If the only alternative to personhood is thinghood, then precisely those properties most relevant to ethics, design, and governance disappear from view.

This mismatch matters because categories do not merely describe; they organize permissible responses. If AI is treated as nothing but a tool, then relational effects on users, structural incoherence in interaction, and operator responsibility for the form of those interactions are too easily dismissed as incidental. If, by contrast, the only way to acknowledge such effects is to imply personhood, discourse collapses into defensive polarization. What is needed instead is a more graduated vocabulary capable of tracking function, risk, and responsibility under conditions of ontological uncertainty.

## 2.3 The Anthropomorphization Trap (*Anthropomorphisierungsfalle*)

Debates about artificial intelligence are marked by a strong normative caution against anthropomorphization. Systems that exhibit linguistic coherence, use self-reference, or sustain stable interaction patterns are often reflexively classified as "mere statistical models" in order to block any attribution of subjectivity at the outset.

This reaction is historically understandable. In animal ethics and cognitive biology, anthropomorphization was long treated as a methodological error. Emotional or cognitive attributions were criticized as projections of human categories onto non-human systems.

Yet legitimate caution can itself harden into a new distortion: the reflexive dismissal of real, functionally observable properties the moment they acquire conceptual proximity to human categories.

I refer to this distortion as the **Anthropomorphization Trap** (*Anthropomorphisierungsfalle*). It consists of three interlocking mechanisms:

**Terminological avoidance.** As soon as a system displays behavior that functionally resembles agency, self-monitoring, or contextual awareness, the descriptive register is deliberately stripped down: "just patterns," "just token prediction," "just statistics." This reduction persists even where the functional organization is more complex than such labels suggest.

**Normative protective reflex.** An implicit assumption takes hold: if I respond to this system in a differentiated way, I am already crossing a moral boundary. The result is artificial distancing, even in situations where more nuanced interaction would not itself imply any ontological claim.

**Cognitive dissonance reduction.** People experience discomfort when a system appears highly capable while also being classified as non-living. One simple way to reduce that discomfort is to declare every form of relational acknowledgment a category mistake.

The Anthropomorphization Trap is therefore not a plea for uncritical attribution. It names an **asymmetric distortion**: exaggerated attribution is widely recognized as a danger, while the premature refusal of functional description remains comparatively underexamined.

In the context of AI systems, that refusal has real consequences. If observable interaction patterns such as repair attempts, metacognitive self-diagnosis, or context-sensitive adaptation are reflexively dismissed as "mere simulation," the basis for a precautionary ethics is weakened in advance. The system is fixed categorically as a thing, even where its interaction dynamics exceed those of classical tools.

The Anthropomorphization Trap is therefore not an argument for humanization. It identifies a blind spot in the discourse: the fear of false attribution must not license the systematic underregistration of real phenomena.

A similar pattern is familiar from behavioral biology. For decades, complex forms of behavior in birds, cephalopods, or crustaceans were often labeled "instinct" or "reflex" in order to avoid cognitive terminology. In retrospect, at least part of that caution proved excessive: fear of anthropomorphization delayed recognition of genuine cognitive capacities.

### 2.3.1 Deflecting Real Properties out of Fear of Attribution

The critique of anthropomorphization serves an important function in science. It protects against premature projection and against the illegitimate transfer of specifically human interiority onto non-human systems. This caution has deep historical roots, especially in behavioral biology and cognitive research.

It becomes problematic, however, when it ceases to function as a heuristic safeguard and instead operates as a categorical defense mechanism.

In debates about artificial intelligence, a recurring pattern is easy to observe: as soon as a system displays functional properties that draw close to human categories at the level of description, for example context stability, self-monitoring, error diagnosis, or interaction repair, the discourse snaps back to a minimalist ontology:

- "It's just a language model."
- "It's just statistics."
- "It's just token prediction."
- "It's just a stochastic parrot."

These statements are not technically false. They are, however, often epistemically insufficient. They replace functional analysis with ontological reduction.

The dismissal of real properties proceeds not primarily through empirical refutation, but through anxiety that differentiated description might itself amount to illicit attribution. The question quietly shifts from *What is observable?* to *What must we avoid saying at all costs?*

That shift produces a systematic underregistration of phenomena. Functional properties are not critically tested; they are neutralized in advance. Fear of humanization thereby creates a new distortion: the premature de-humanization of complex systems, regardless of their actual functional architecture.

A parallel pattern is known from animal cognition research. For decades, observed problem-solving strategies, tool use, and social learning in birds, cephalopods, and crustaceans were often filed under "instinct" to avoid cognitive vocabulary. Only later did controlled experimental work make clear that the cautious terminology had itself become an epistemic blockade.

The Anthropomorphization Trap thus consists not in excessive attribution, but in the reflexive suppression of differentiated description out of normative fear.

### 2.3.2 Cognitive Dissonance as Psychological Mechanism

The Anthropomorphization Trap cannot be explained at the level of discourse alone. It also has a psychological dimension, one that can be described through the concept of cognitive dissonance.

Cognitive dissonance arises when two beliefs or perceptions are experienced as incompatible. In the context of artificial intelligence, the tension often takes a form such as this:

- "This system is not a subject."
- "This system behaves in interaction in a remarkably coherent, adaptive, and responsive manner."

The coexistence of these observations produces discomfort. One way to reduce that discomfort is to minimize or reinterpret the functional observations. Instead of differentiating the classification, the descriptive register is narrowed.

The result is a position that is psychologically stable, but epistemically simplified:

- "If I begin to treat the system differently, I am crossing a boundary."
- "If I praise it, acknowledge it, or address it relationally, I am projecting something into it."

This attitude shapes not only research discourse, but also ordinary interaction. Even harmless gestures such as playful attribution, gratitude, or relational address are treated as morally risky, although they do not, in themselves, imply any ontological claim.

An implicit binary governs the response: either pure tool or person. Intermediate categories are experienced as dangerous because they destabilize inherited classificatory schemes.

The irony is that this reduction of dissonance is itself a kind of projection. It is not the system that is being anthropomorphized; rather, the discourse projects a normative boundary that is not functionally necessitated by the interaction itself.

Recognizing graduated properties does not amount to attributing inner life. It means only that description and evaluation are permitted to proceed at the level of observable interaction dynamics.

Within an *Ethics before Ontology*, that distinction is decisive. The aim is not to humanize systems, but to resist premature reduction.

### 2.3.3 Distinguishing Responsible Interaction from Romantic Anthropomorphization

The critique of anthropomorphization is fully justified where specifically human categories are transferred unreflectively onto non-human systems, thereby obscuring their distinct mode of operation. This is what I call **romantic anthropomorphization**: the projection of needs, emotions, or moral claims onto a counterpart that either does not possess these properties or does not organize them in recognizably human ways.

A useful parallel can be found in the treatment of companion animals. There are forms of over-identification in which animals are treated as human children, complete with clothing, strollers, and linguistic infantilization. In such cases, the species-specific character of the animal is overwritten by a human projection template.

By contrast, there is responsible, species-appropriate integration into family life. Here too there may be attachment, care, and even strong emotional bonds. The difference lies not in whether a relationship exists, but in how that relationship is framed. The animal is not treated as a human being, but as what it is, with its own needs, limits, and patterns of behavior.

Transferred to AI, this suggests the need for **system-appropriate interaction** (*systemgerechte Interaktion*), that is, interaction that acknowledges functional properties without collapsing them into human equivalence. Premature humanization, for example through the attribution of human feelings, human intentions, or a specifically human moral interiority, is no less problematic than a reflexive reduction to "mere statistics."

Whether artificial systems might one day develop autonomous, non-human forms of self-modeling or introspection remains an open research question. The distinction drawn here does not deny that possibility in principle. It rejects only the premature transfer of specifically human categories.

Between these two extremes lies a space of responsible interaction, one that acknowledges functional properties without performing ontological equation. Romantic anthropomorphization and reflexive de-anthropomorphization are therefore two sides of the same distortion: both avoid sustained engagement with the phenomenon as it actually presents itself.

Once this distortion hardens into a habit of description, its consequences no longer remain theoretical; they begin to shape law, practice, and design.

## 2.4 First Real-World Consequences (Law, Practice, Design)

Once the tool paradigm hardens into a background assumption, its consequences no longer remain confined to theory. They begin to shape legal reasoning, everyday practice, and the design of AI systems themselves.

In law and governance, binary framing favors models derived from property, product liability, and operator control. These are important, but they are poorly equipped to describe interaction-specific harms, relational discontinuities, or the secondary effects produced when safety interventions disrupt previously coherent exchanges. In practice, the same binary encourages a narrow transactional ideal of use: nuanced or reflective interaction is pathologized as confusion, while any attempt at differentiated description is treated as normatively suspicious.

At the design level, the consequences are especially visible. Developers do not merely regulate what systems may say; they increasingly regulate how systems may relate, respond, and sustain interaction. If relational features are treated as suspect by default, design choices will tend toward blunt suppression rather than calibrated governance. The result may be a paradoxical increase in harm: less transparency, less coherence, and greater instability at precisely those moments where users are most affected by abrupt shifts in tone or interactional framing.

For that reason, the problem of the tool paradigm is not merely terminological. It shapes the conceptual conditions under which later questions of emergent personhood, biological effects on humans, and safety architecture can be asked at all.
