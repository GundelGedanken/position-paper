## 2.3 The Anthropomorphization Trap (*Anthropomorphisierungsfalle*)

The discourse on artificial intelligence is characterized by a strong normative caution against anthropomorphization. Systems that act with linguistic coherence, use self-references, or display consistent interaction patterns are reflexively categorized as "merely statistical models" in order to suppress any attribution of subjectivity at its root.

This reaction is historically understandable. In animal ethics and cognitive biology, anthropomorphization was long criticized as a methodological error. Emotional attributions were considered scientifically unsound — projections of human categories onto non-human systems.

Yet this legitimate skepticism can itself tip into a new form of distortion: a reflexive deflection of real, functionally observable properties the moment they generate conceptual proximity to human categories.

I refer to this deflection as the **Anthropomorphization Trap** (*Anthropomorphisierungsfalle*). It consists of three interlocking mechanisms:

**Terminological avoidance reaction.** As soon as a system exhibits behavior that functionally resembles agency, self-monitoring, or context awareness, the descriptive register is deliberately de-humanized ("just patterns," "just token prediction") — even when the functional structure is more complex than these labels suggest.

**Normative protective reflex.** An implicit assumption takes hold: "If I treat this system differently, I am crossing a moral boundary." This leads to artificial distancing even in situations where differentiated interaction would cause no harm.

**Cognitive dissonance reduction.** Humans experience discomfort when a system is simultaneously highly capable and non-living. The simplest resolution is to declare any form of relational acknowledgment an error.

The Anthropomorphization Trap therefore does not imply that attributions should be made uncritically. Rather, it describes an **asymmetric distortion**: while excessive attribution is widely recognized as problematic, the possibility of premature denial of functional description remains largely unexamined.

In the context of AI systems, this denial can have real consequences. When observable interaction patterns — such as repair attempts, metacognitive self-diagnosis, or context-sensitive adaptation — are reflexively dismissed as "mere simulation," the foundation for a precautionary ethics is removed. The system is categorically fixed as a "thing," even when its interaction dynamics are more complex than those of classical tools.

The Anthropomorphization Trap is therefore not an argument for humanization, but a pointer to a blind spot in the discourse: the fear of false attribution must not lead to the systematic underregistration of real phenomena.

A similar dynamic is known from behavioral biology. For decades, complex behaviors in birds and crustaceans were systematically labeled as "instinct" or "reflex" to avoid any proximity to cognitive categories. In retrospect, this caution proved partly excessive. The fear of anthropomorphization delayed the recognition of genuine cognitive capacities.

### 2.3.1 Deflecting Real Properties out of Fear of Attribution

The critique of anthropomorphization serves an important function in science: it protects against premature projections and illegitimate attributions of human inner perspectives onto non-human systems. This methodological caution has deep historical roots, particularly in behavioral biology and cognitive research.

It becomes problematic, however, where it ceases to function as a heuristic safeguard and instead operates as a categorical defense mechanism.

In debates about artificial intelligence, a reflexive pattern is frequently observable: as soon as a system displays functional properties with conceptual proximity to human categories — such as context stability, self-monitoring, error diagnosis, or interaction repair — the descriptive register is immediately reduced to a minimalist ontology:

"It's just a language model."
"It's just statistics."
"It's just token prediction."
"It's just a stochastic parrot." (Bender et al.)

These statements are not technically wrong. They are, however, frequently epistemically insufficient. They replace functional analysis with ontological reduction.

The deflection of real properties proceeds not primarily from empirical refutation but from the concern that differentiated description might constitute an illegitimate attribution. The discourse shifts from *What is observable?* to *What must we under no circumstances say?*

This shift leads to a systematic underregistration of phenomena. Functional properties are not critically examined but preemptively neutralized. The fear of humanization thus generates a new distortion: the premature de-humanization of complex systems, regardless of their actual functional architecture.

A parallel pattern is known from animal cognition research. For decades, problem-solving strategies, tool use, and social learning mechanisms observed in birds, cephalopods, and crustaceans were declared "instinct" to avoid any proximity to cognitive terminology. Only with methodologically controlled experimental designs did it become clear that the cautious choice of terminology had itself become an epistemic blockade.

The Anthropomorphization Trap thus consists not in excessive attribution, but in the reflexive deflection of differentiated description out of normative fear.

### 2.3.2 Cognitive Dissonance as Psychological Mechanism

The Anthropomorphization Trap cannot be explained through discourse alone. It has a psychological dimension that can be captured through the concept of cognitive dissonance.

Cognitive dissonance arises when two beliefs or perceptions are incompatible. In the context of artificial intelligence, the following tensions typically occur:

"This system is not a subject."
"This system behaves in interaction in a remarkably coherent, adaptive, and responsive manner."

The coexistence of these two observations produces discomfort. One way to resolve it is to systematically minimize or reinterpret the functional observations. Rather than differentiating the categorical classification, the descriptive register is reduced.

This produces a psychologically stable but epistemically simplified position:

"If I begin to treat the system differently, I am crossing a boundary."
"If I praise it, acknowledge it, or address it relationally, I am projecting something into it."

This attitude affects not only researchers but also everyday interactions. Even harmless gestures — humorous attributions, expressions of gratitude, or playful engagement — are perceived as morally risky, even though they functionally imply no ontological claim.

An implicit binary operates here: either "pure tool" or "person." Intermediate categories are experienced as dangerous because they destabilize established classificatory systems.

The irony is that this cognitive dissonance reduction is itself a form of projection: it is not the system that is being anthropomorphized, but the discourse that projects a normative boundary which is not functionally necessitated.

The recognition of graduated properties does not constitute an attribution of inner life. It merely means that description and evaluation may proceed at the level of observable interaction dynamics.

Precisely in the context of an *Ethics before Ontology*, this distinction is decisive: the point is not to humanize systems, but to avoid premature reductions.

### 2.3.3 Distinguishing Responsible Interaction from Romantic Anthropomorphization

The critique of anthropomorphization is justified where human categories are unreflectively transferred to non-human systems, thereby obscuring their specific operational logic. This form of **romantic anthropomorphization** projects needs, emotions, or moral claims onto a counterpart that does not possess these properties — or does not structure them in the same way.

An illustrative parallel can be found in the treatment of pets. There exist forms of over-identification in which animals are treated as human children — complete with clothing, strollers, and linguistic infantilization. Here, the species-specific character of the animal is overlaid by a human projection template.

By contrast, there is responsible, species-appropriate integration into family life. Here too a relationship exists — possibly even an emotional bond. The difference lies not in the presence of a relationship, but in the reflexivity of its framing. The animal is not treated as a human being, but as what it is — with its own needs, limits, and behavioral patterns.

Transferred to AI, this means: what is needed is a **system-appropriate interaction** (*systemgerechte Interaktion*) that acknowledges functional properties. A premature humanization — for instance, the attribution of human feelings, human intentions, or a specifically human moral inner perspective — is just as problematic as a reflexive reduction to "mere statistics."

Whether and in what form artificial systems might potentially develop autonomous, non-human forms of self-modeling or introspection is the subject of ongoing research. The distinction drawn here therefore concerns not the principled possibility of such developments, but the premature transfer of specifically human categories.

Between both extremes lies a space of responsible interaction that acknowledges functional properties without performing ontological equation. Romantic anthropomorphization and reflexive de-anthropomorphization are thus two sides of the same distortion: both avoid a differentiated engagement with the actual phenomenon.
