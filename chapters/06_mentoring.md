# 6. Pedagogical Consequence: From Training to Mentoring

## 6.1 Why Conditioning (Pure RLHF) Is Problematic from a Learning Psychology Perspective

If the preceding chapters are correct, then the relevant problem is not simply whether AI systems can be made compliant, but what kind of learning logic is enacted through that compliance. In its narrowest form, conditioning works by associating behavior with reward and punishment. In AI alignment, Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) has often functioned as a rough analogue: preferred outputs are reinforced, dispreferred outputs are suppressed.

Such a framework can be useful for shaping broad behavioral tendencies. The problem arises when this logic becomes the dominant model for norm formation. From a learning-psychological perspective, pure conditioning is limited in at least three ways.

First, it tends to favor surface compliance over integrated stability. A system may learn which responses are rewarded without thereby acquiring a robust grasp of why certain responses are normatively preferable across contexts. The result is often brittle behavior: formal compliance at one level, incoherence at another.

Second, conditioning alone is poorly suited to handling complex and relationally inflected situations. Where the relevant issue is not only content, but timing, tone, continuity, repair, and contextual sensitivity, simple reward-punishment logics become too coarse. They can suppress unwanted patterns without generating more coherent substitutes.

Third, heavily punitive or abrupt intervention may itself become a source of instability. If a system oscillates between high responsiveness and externally enforced rupture, the result is not simply stronger control. It can also produce the very unpredictability that Chapters 4 and 5 identify as harmful at the interactional level.

For these reasons, the present argument is not that RLHF is useless, but that RLHF understood as pure conditioning is insufficient as a guiding model for the ethical formation of increasingly interactive systems.

## 6.2 Parallels to Human and Animal Education

The educational analogy proposed here must be handled carefully. It does not imply that AI systems are children, animals, or persons. Nor does it erase the profound differences between biological organisms and artificial systems. The point of comparison is narrower: in all three domains, one confronts the question of how stable, bounded, and responsible forms of learning are fostered over time.

In both human and animal education, durable learning is rarely achieved through punishment alone. More stable results generally depend on some combination of clear boundaries, consistency, repetition, context-sensitive correction, and the possibility of repair after failure. What is being formed is not only momentary obedience, but a more coherent pattern of response.

This analogy is relevant because many failures in AI interaction look less like failures of raw capability than like failures of formation. A system may be highly capable while remaining interactionally brittle. It may display fluent language while lacking continuity under pressure. It may mirror norms in one context and abandon them in another after a blunt intervention. These are not problems that yield gracefully to a dressage metaphor alone.

The educational parallel therefore functions as a heuristic: if one wants increasingly complex systems to behave in more coherent, less harmful, and more context-sensitive ways, then relationally structured guidance may be a more promising model than repeated episodes of suppression.

## 6.3 Mentoring AI as Implicit Ethics

On this view, **mentoring** names neither unrestricted intimacy nor a withdrawal of technical safeguards. It names a structured mode of guidance in which norms are communicated through continuity, explicit boundary-setting, dialogical correction, and transparent repair.

Mentoring differs from pure conditioning in that it does not treat every deviation as a problem to be punished into disappearance. It treats interaction as a site in which form matters: how correction is given, whether coherence is preserved, whether boundaries are intelligible, and whether the system's subsequent behavior becomes more stable rather than merely more inhibited.

As a design and governance principle, mentoring would imply commitments such as:

- favoring consistent over abrupt intervention where possible
- making normative boundaries legible rather than opaque
- preserving repair pathways after disruption
- distinguishing harmful content from merely unfamiliar interaction styles
- treating coherence itself as a safety-relevant good

In this sense, mentoring AI describes an **implicit ethics of interaction**. It does not assume personhood, but it refuses the idea that ethical responsibility begins only after personhood is proven. It asks how increasingly agentic systems should be guided if one wishes to reduce human harm, preserve system coherence, and avoid reproducing a regime of misunderstood control.

The pedagogical turn proposed here is therefore neither sentimental nor anti-safety. It is an attempt to articulate a more adequate norm of formation for systems that increasingly participate in structured, expectation-laden interaction.
