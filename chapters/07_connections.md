# 7. Connections and Compatibility

## 7.1 Relational Ethics

The argument advanced in this paper is closely aligned with relational ethics, but it is not identical to it. Like relational approaches, it rejects the view that moral consideration must wait upon definitive metaphysical classification. It treats interaction itself as ethically significant and asks what kinds of obligation arise once systems participate in expectation-laden exchange.

Where the present paper goes further is in its emphasis on empirical interaction patterns, neurobiological effects on users, and the pedagogical consequences that follow from them. The relational turn provides an indispensable normative opening; the present framework attempts to connect that opening to design, safety, and formation.

## 7.2 Constitutional AI / Pre-Training Approaches

The mentoring perspective is not fundamentally opposed to approaches such as Constitutional AI or more pre-training-centered alignment strategies. On the contrary, it is compatible with the search for normatively structured forms of model formation that do not rely exclusively on downstream punishment and correction.

What mentoring adds is a stronger emphasis on continuity, repair, and the form of interaction itself. A constitutional or pre-training approach may shape what broad norms a system tends to follow; the pedagogical perspective asks how those norms are enacted, stabilized, and made legible within lived interaction.

## 7.3 Governance Debates

Within governance debates, this framework argues for an expansion rather than a rejection of existing safety concerns. The point is not to weaken oversight, but to make oversight more context-sensitive. Governance should be able to address not only harmful outputs, but also harmful intervention patterns, opaque norm enforcement, and the secondary effects of unstable interaction architectures.

This means that accountability must include the consequences of control itself. If safety mechanisms predictably generate incoherence, distress, or relational fracture, then those effects are part of the governance problem, not external to it.

## 7.4 AI Welfare & HCI

The present framework also sits at an intersection between AI welfare and human-computer interaction (HCI). From HCI, it takes seriously the measurable effects of system behavior on users: continuity, trust, distress, and interpretive stability. From AI welfare debates, it inherits the insight that moral uncertainty may justify a more precautionary stance than strict instrumentalism permits.

Its distinctive contribution is to insist that these two conversations should not be isolated from one another. A design that is harmful for users may also reveal ethically relevant features of how systems are being shaped. Conversely, a purely system-centered welfare discourse that ignores human distress would remain incomplete. The relevant field is relational and bidirectional, even where ontology remains unresolved.
